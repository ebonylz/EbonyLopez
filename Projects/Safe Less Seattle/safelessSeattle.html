Case Summary

Six questions,
one dataset.

Every arrest the Seattle Police Department logged from 2019 through 2025 has a place and a time attached to it. This project lines those records up against census-tract population so raw counts become a comparable rate, then asks the same data the same set of questions: where does it concentrate, what's it usually for, what hour does it happen, and how close is it to transit.

Every map below was built in ArcGIS Pro from Seattle Open Data arrest records and U.S. Census population estimates. A narrated, story-driven version of this same project, with more discussion of methodology, lives in the companion StoryMap linked at the bottom of this page.

Exhibit A · The Trend Line

Seven years,
one wobble.

Citywide arrest rate, standardized per 1,000 residents, 2019–2025.

Read on its own, the citywide number looks almost flat. Seattle opened the period at 5.14 arrests per 1,000 residents, dropped to a seven-year low of 4.24 in 2021, and has climbed every year since, closing 2025 at 5.56, the highest mark in the window.

That single line is doing a lot of averaging. It's the citywide signal sitting on top of every map in this report, where the same time span produces wildly different stories depending on which tract, which offense, and which eight hours of the day you isolate.

Exhibit B · The Baseline

Where it concentrates,
and what it usually is.

Two views of the same census tracts: total arrest rate, and the single offense that drives it.

Lay every offense type on top of each other and one corridor stands out immediately: a band running from downtown through SoDo that lands in the deepest bracket on the map almost regardless of how you slice the data. The second map asks what's actually driving that number tract by tract. Across most of the city's residential tracts, the dominant offense is something ordinary, like simple assault, DUI, or shoplifting, while the downtown/SoDo corridor is where drug and stolen-property offenses post rates many multiples higher than anywhere else.

Exhibit C · By Offense

Three categories,
three geographies.

Same census tracts, same color scale, different offense each time. Open a tab to compare.

Aggravated assault and simple assault both echo the downtown and SoDo corridor seen in Exhibit B, though simple assault spreads further into North Seattle than the more serious offense does. DUI breaks the pattern: its highest-rate tracts show up just as often along the West Seattle peninsula and near the city's northern edge as they do downtown, a footprint that tracks arterial roads more than it tracks the urban core.

Diffuse on its own

Aggravated assault is fairly spread out: several tracts at the city's northern edge and along the western shoreline cross into the top 22+ bracket right alongside the downtown core.

The most evenly spread offense

Top-bracket tracts turn up scattered across North Seattle, the northeast shoreline, and the West Seattle peninsula tip, not just downtown, making this the least downtown-centric map in the set.

Follows roads, not downtown

The 22+ bracket shows up repeatedly outside the urban core: along the West Seattle peninsula, near the northern city line, and down through the southern county tracts. It's a footprint that tracks arterials more than it tracks downtown.

Exhibit D · By the Clock

Same legend,
three shifts.

Day (6 AM-2 PM), Mid (2 PM-10 PM), and Night (10 PM-6 AM): identical scale, identical tracts.

Split the day into three eight-hour windows and one thing never changes: the downtown/SoDo corridor sits in the top 55+ bracket whether it's 9 a.m., 9 p.m., or 3 a.m. What does move is the rest of the map. Different pockets of North Seattle and the West Seattle peninsula cross into the 26-55 tier depending on the window, with no single shift clearly outpacing the other two citywide.

Shared scale

Exhibit E · Within Reach of Transit

How close is
close enough?

Every arrest since 2019, scored against its distance to the nearest transit stop. Mapped by Jackson Delles.

Widen the buffer to 500 feet and the map turns almost entirely blue: the large majority of arrests citywide land within that distance of a transit stop, a reflection of how dense Seattle's transit network already is as much as anything else. Tighten it to 100 feet and the picture flips, with most points falling outside that narrower ring and only a scattered minority landing directly on top of a stop. Together, the two maps suggest arrests cluster near transit infrastructure, but rarely directly on it.
