Why this matters When most people think of 911, they picture violent crime. But Seattle's call data tells a different story. The most common calls involve disturbances, suspicious circumstances, public assistance requests, and traffic-related incidents rather than major violent crimes. Understanding what residents actually call 911 for provides a clearer picture of how emergency services are used and what kinds of problems communities face every day.

In 2020, Seattle was at a turning point. The COVID-19 pandemic reshaped daily life, and the nationwide response to George Floyd's death sparked renewed debates about policing and public safety. Five years later, those conversations continue, while emergency call data provides an opportunity to examine how community needs have changed over time.

This project explores Seattle's 911 call records from 2020 and 2025 to answer three questions: What kinds of incidents generate the most calls? How have those patterns changed after accounting for population growth? And when are people most likely to seek emergency assistance? Together, these visualizations reveal a broader picture of public safety that extends beyond crime statistics alone.

Background: How 911 Works in Seattle

Understanding Seattle's 911 System

Before diving into the data, it helps to know how Seattle classifies and responds to emergency calls.

When to Call 911

Call 911 for any in-progress emergency or crime: an assault, medical emergency, fire, or gas leak. For non-urgent situations (a car prowl that happened overnight, noise complaints, parking issues), use the non-emergency line at 206-625-5011 or file a report online.

Source: With CARE, Seattle.gov (2025)

How Calls Are Categorized

What you see in the data is a "clearance description," not the initial 911 report. After an officer responds and assesses the situation, they assign a clearance code reflecting what they actually found. The same caller report can produce different codes depending on what was observed on arrival.

Source: Seattle Breaking, SPD Call Types Explained (2026)

Call Priority Levels

Every 911 call is assigned a priority based on how much risk it poses to public safety. Here is what each level means:

  • Priority 1 Critical incidents posing an immediate threat to human life or involving significant bodily harm (e.g., shootings, robberies, violent assaults).
  • Priority 2 Active incidents that could escalate; crimes where enforcement is still possible; and most service requests from other agencies (e.g., shoplifts, property damage, active disturbances).
  • Priority 3 Incidents that do not meet Priority 1 or 2 criteria but may require officer investigation or action (e.g., cold reports, follow-up checks).
  • Priority 4 Non-critical service requests such as found property or parking complaints.
  • Priority 5 Lowest-priority calls, typically administrative or informational in nature, such as requests for documentation or officer-generated activity with no immediate public safety need.

Source: With CARE, Seattle.gov (2025)

What the Call Types in This Data Mean

The categories below are the exact event groups that appear in Seattle's 911 call data. Each is a clearance description assigned after an officer responds, not necessarily what the caller originally reported. (Seattle Breaking)

Disturbance Situations that called for police but did not rise to a specific crime: verbal arguments, loud music, group disputes. Very common, often non-violent, and frequently resolved without arrest.
Suspicious Circumstances A resident called to report something they found suspicious: a parked vehicle, a person acting oddly, someone ringing doorbells. Does not mean officers found criminal activity. The vast majority resolve as no incident.
Assaults Physical attacks, ranging from minor altercations to aggravated assault involving weapons. Includes domestic violence assaults and non-domestic incidents.
Assist Public General requests for police assistance that do not fit a specific crime category, such as helping someone who is lost, locked out, or in need of a welfare check.
Traffic Collisions, DUIs, hit-and-runs, and other vehicle-related incidents. Includes both injury and non-injury crashes. Police and fire often respond to the same incident separately.
Prowler Reports of a person suspected of trespassing or lurking around a property with possible intent to commit a crime. Often difficult to verify and frequently resolved with no suspect found.
Assist Other Agency Calls where SPD provides backup or support to another agency, such as the fire department, King County, or Washington State Patrol.
Theft Stolen property, ranging from shoplifting to auto theft to bicycle theft. SPD distinguishes these sub-types because response patterns differ significantly.
Burglary Unauthorized entry into a building with intent to commit a crime, almost always theft. Includes both residential and commercial burglaries, and "cold" reports filed after the fact.
Automobiles Calls specifically related to vehicles: car prowls, auto theft follow-up, abandoned vehicles, or vehicle-related disturbances not classified under Traffic.
Property Destruction (Damage) Vandalism, graffiti, or damage to property. Often reported after the fact rather than in progress. Can be filed online in many cases.
Mischief & Nuisance Low-level disruptive behavior that does not meet the threshold of a disturbance or specific crime: harassment, minor public disorder, or repeated nuisance activity.

Source: Seattle Breaking, SPD Call Types Explained (2026)

Section 2: How Calls Changed After 2020

Emergency call patterns shifted after 2020, even after adjusting for population growth

Seattle's population grew between 2020 and 2025, so raw call counts alone can be misleading. Each row below shows the same call category in 2020 (gold dot) and 2025 (purple dot), standardized to calls per 100,000 residents. A line connects the two years: movement to the right means a higher per-resident rate, while movement to the left means fewer calls per person. Hover to compare exact values.

2020 2025
Reading this chart: This chart compares each call category's rate per 100,000 residents in 2020 and 2025. Categories where the purple dot is farther right than the gold dot became more common per resident, while categories where the purple dot moved left became less common. Assist Public, Assist Other Agency, Traffic, and Automobiles increased, while Disturbance, Prowler, Burglary, and Suspicious Circumstances declined. This shows that Seattle's 911 demand did not change evenly across every call type.

Which call types grew or fell the most per 100,000 residents?

This chart turns the comparison above into a single change score for each category. Green bars show call types that became more common per resident between 2020 and 2025, while red bars show call types that became less common. Because the chart adjusts for population growth, these changes reflect shifts in call rates rather than just more people living in the city.

Reading this chart: Assist Public had the largest increase, followed by Assist Other Agency, Narcotics, Traffic, Shots, and Automobiles. Disturbance had the largest decline, followed by Prowler, Burglary, Suspicious Circumstances, and Property Destruction. The farther a bar stretches away from zero, the larger the change in that category's per-resident call rate.
So what? These two charts show that Seattle's emergency call patterns changed in uneven ways after 2020. Some public-assistance and agency-support categories became more common per resident, while several disturbance and property-related categories became less common. This matters because it shows that emergency response demand is not just about total call volume. The types of calls are changing too, which means staffing, resources, and public safety planning need to pay attention to the mix of calls, not only the overall number.

So what? The biggest changes did not occur in the highest-priority emergencies. This highlights how emergency systems spend significant time responding to problems that are important to residents but are not always immediate life-safety emergencies.

Section 4: When Are People Calling?

Hour-by-hour call density across both years

This heatmap shows all calls per 100,000 residents by hour and year. Darker cells indicate higher rates. The pattern reveals that call volume is not evenly spread across the day. Both years show lower call rates in the early morning and higher call rates from midday into the evening.

Reading this chart: The heatmap gives a quick overview of when Seattle’s emergency call system is busiest. Lighter cells show slower hours, while darker cells show higher call rates per 100,000 residents. In both 2020 and 2025, the quietest period is generally early morning, while demand rises later in the day. This helps show that emergency calls follow a daily rhythm instead of happening evenly across all hours.

How Seattle's daily call patterns looked during the pandemic

This chart breaks down 2020 calls by hour and call type. Because 2020 was shaped by COVID-19 restrictions and changes in daily routines, it works as a comparison point for understanding how emergency call timing looked during a disrupted year. Use the dropdown to see when each call type peaked.

Reading this chart: The x-axis shows each hour from midnight to 11 PM, while the y-axis shows the number of calls recorded during that hour in 2020. Many call types still follow a clear daily pattern, with lower activity in the early morning and higher activity around midday or evening. How daily call patterns changed by 2025

This chart uses the same hourly breakdown for 2025, making it easier to compare against the 2020 pattern above. Switching between call types shows which patterns stayed similar and which ones shifted as Seattle moved out of the pandemic period. (With CARE, Seattle.gov)

Reading this chart: In 2025, Seattle’s emergency calls still show clear differences by call type. Many categories, including Assaults, Theft, Prowler, Assist Public, Assist Other Agency, and Automobiles, reach their highest levels around noon. Disturbance, Suspicious Circumstances, and Traffic calls show stronger evening peaks, with activity building into the late afternoon and evening. Across most categories, the lowest call volume is still between roughly 2 AM and 5 AM. This shows that the type of incident matters when trying to understand when people are most likely to call 911.
Comparing 2020 and 2025: The main daily rhythm stayed fairly consistent across both years: calls were generally lowest in the early morning and higher later in the day. However, the timing of some call types shifted. Traffic calls peaked around 5 PM in 2020 but moved later in 2025, while Disturbance and Suspicious Circumstances remained strongly evening-based in both years. Midday peaks also stayed important for categories like Theft, Prowler, and Assist Public. Overall, the comparison suggests that the pandemic changed some parts of Seattle’s emergency call patterns, but many daily rhythms stayed stable.
Section 5: What Does It All Mean?

Seattle's emergency calls reveal a broader picture of community needs

Takeaway Seattle's 911 data shows that emergency demand is more complex than many people assume. The most common calls are not shootings or other major crimes, but disturbances, suspicious circumstances, public assistance requests, traffic incidents, and other everyday concerns. Looking across both 2020 and 2025 reveals that emergency response is shaped by a wide range of community needs that extend beyond traditional crime response.

The data also shows that Seattle's emergency call patterns changed between 2020 and 2025. Some call categories became more common on a per-resident basis, while others declined. Priority-level trends suggest that lower-priority service requests grew while the highest-priority calls became less common. At the same time, many daily call patterns remained consistent, with activity generally increasing through the afternoon and evening before declining overnight.

Together, these visualizations provide a clearer picture of how residents use emergency services and when they seek help. While 911 is often associated with major emergencies, the data suggests that much of the system's workload comes from everyday issues that affect quality of life, public safety, and community well-being. Understanding these patterns can help residents and decision-makers better understand how emergency resources are used and how community needs continue to evolve over time.
