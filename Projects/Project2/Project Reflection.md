# Project Reflection

For this project, I wanted to explore what Seattle's 911 call data could reveal about public safety beyond crime statistics alone. I chose to compare 2020 and 2025 because 2020 was heavily impacted by COVID-19 and the public conversations around policing following George Floyd's death. I was interested in seeing whether emergency call patterns had changed over time and whether those changes remained after accounting for population growth.

One of my main goals was to create a data story rather than a dashboard. Instead of simply showing charts, I organized the project into sections that gradually answered different questions. First, I examined which types of calls were most common. Then I looked at how those patterns changed between 2020 and 2025 using population-adjusted rates. After that, I explored call priority levels and finally analyzed when people were most likely to call 911 throughout the day. Structuring the project this way helped create a narrative that built from broad trends to more detailed insights.

One of the biggest challenges was working with the size of the dataset. At first, I wanted to analyze all available 911 call data from 2020 through 2025. However, the dataset contained hundreds of thousands of records, and trying to load the entire time range into my visualizations created performance issues on my computer. Pages became slow to load, charts took much longer to render, and it made development difficult. Because of this, I decided to focus on comparing 2020 and 2025. This still allowed me to examine meaningful changes over time while keeping the project manageable and responsive.

Another challenge involved incorporating geographic information. I originally planned to include maps showing where calls were occurring throughout Seattle, but I ran into difficulties both with mapping the data and finding population data that aligned with Seattle Police Department precinct boundaries. While citywide population data was readily available, finding reliable population estimates that matched precinct boundaries was much more difficult than I expected. Since I wanted to compare areas fairly, I decided not to include a precinct-level analysis and instead focused on citywide population-adjusted comparisons.

I also had to determine how to compare the two years fairly. Since Seattle's population changed between 2020 and 2025, using raw call counts alone could be misleading. To address this, I incorporated population data and standardized several visualizations to calls per 100,000 residents. This allowed me to focus on actual changes in call behavior rather than changes caused simply by population growth.

I completed all aspects of this project independently, including data preparation, analysis, narrative development, visualization design, and implementation using HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and D3. Throughout the design process, I focused on making the visualizations interactive and understandable for a public audience. The dropdown menus, tooltips, explanatory text, and callout sections were all designed to help readers understand not only what the data showed, but why those findings mattered.

Overall, I think the project successfully demonstrates that Seattle's emergency response system handles a much broader range of situations than many people might expect. The data showed that disturbances, suspicious circumstances, public assistance requests, and traffic-related incidents make up a large portion of emergency demand, while patterns also changed in different ways after 2020. If I continued working on this project, I would like to revisit the mapping component, find more detailed population data, and explore additional years of 911 call records to better understand longer-term trends. This project helped me think more critically about how public datasets can be used to tell stories about community needs, public services, and the challenges cities face when responding to those needs.
